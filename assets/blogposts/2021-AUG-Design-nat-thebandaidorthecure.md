NAT - The Bandaid or The Cure?

I've applied a fair amount of NAT (Network Address Translation) as an engineer and I often find
myself feeling disguisting afterwards. Sure, the business cheers with joy because it works and
their requirements are met but as an engineer I feel like I loaded a sawdof shotgun and just unloaded
it onto my network. I wanted to take some time to figure out why I felt awful while the CEO pop's 
champagne next to my desk.

When I got into the networking space it was drilled into me that NAT is often the bandaid within a
network design and what I quickly experienced as I moved between projects was that single NAT / double NAT
(sometimes triple NAT) was often the status quo. Because of my bias I was immediately on the opposing
side of the design complaining that we don't have a clear "end-to-end" path of connectivity from a
Layer 3 perspective. 

It has only been recently that I've taken a step back from the engineering mindset and looked in the
eyes of Architecture and Design that makes me understand that NAT isn't that evil. There are some
very valid use cases for NAT which I can appreciate now that I understand that the goal within an
Enterprise business isn't to have the sexiest technology stack. The goal as I see it is to have a 
technology stack that can function effectively to meet the needs of the business.

I have quickly realised how closed minded I can be when I look at a technology purely from an 
engineering perspective. It made me release why I often got push back from some of my technology-focused 
ideas because although it solved problems within a logical perspective, it didn't actually provide a tangible 
benefit for the business.
